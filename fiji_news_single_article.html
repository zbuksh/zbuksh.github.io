BSP Fiji Fined FJD 10,000 for Alleged Breach of Consumer Protection Policy Following ATM Malfunction

BSP Fiji has been issued a fine of FJD 10,000 by the Reserve Bank of Fiji (RBF) in this scenario for an alleged breach of the Policy for the Protection and Fair Treatment of Financial Consumers following the handling of an ATM malfunction complaint. According to the enforcement action, the incident involved an ATM transaction dispute where a customer's account was debited despite cash not being dispensed. The regulator allegedly found that the complaint was not resolved within the expected timeframe and that communication with the affected customer did not meet consumer protection standards..

By Mereani Vula•12 July 2026•4 min read•News

Suva’s newest coastal market opened to the public on Sunday morning, giving small-scale farmers, fishers and food vendors a purpose-built place to trade near the city’s busy waterfront.

The bright, naturally ventilated facility has space for more than 120 stallholders and includes chilled storage, wash-down areas, accessible walkways and covered loading zones. City officials said the project was designed around direct feedback from vendors who had spent years working in temporary or weather-exposed locations.

From before sunrise, families arrived carrying baskets of cassava, leafy greens, tropical fruit and freshly caught seafood. By mid-morning, the central aisle was filled with shoppers, while live music and community demonstrations marked the official opening.

“This market is more than a building. It is a safer, stronger platform for families who keep our local food economy moving.”
— Market committee representative

A better place to trade

Vendor Litia Tawake said the covered stalls would make a meaningful difference during periods of heavy rain. She previously had to move produce several times a day to protect it from changing weather, a disruption that often led to damaged stock and lost income.

The market’s layout separates fresh produce, seafood and prepared food areas. Dedicated waste stations and water-saving fixtures have also been installed, with vendors receiving short training sessions on hygiene, recycling and emergency procedures before opening day.

Market management said priority had been given to registered microbusinesses and family-run operators from communities in and around Suva. A rotating section will allow rural cooperatives to book short-term stalls when they travel to the capital.

Focus on access and resilience

The project was developed to remain usable during difficult weather, with improved drainage, raised electrical fittings and sheltered pedestrian access. Wide aisles, ramp entry points and clearly marked service areas are intended to make the facility easier to navigate for older people, parents with prams and customers with disabilities.

Transport connections will be reviewed during the first three months of operation. Officials are also considering a weekend shuttle stop and designated pick-up zones to reduce congestion at peak times.

What happens next

For the opening month, the facility will operate seven days a week with extended hours on Fridays and Saturdays. Management will gather feedback through vendor meetings and a public survey, then adjust stall allocations, cleaning schedules and traffic arrangements where needed.

Local business groups welcomed the opening but said long-term success would depend on fair stall fees, reliable maintenance and transparent management. They also encouraged shoppers to support vendors consistently after the opening celebrations.

As afternoon trade began, vendors said the early response had been encouraging. For many, the most important change was simple: a permanent place where customers could find them, whatever the weather.
